Mississippi Title Search: Navigating the Magnolia State's Requirements

📅 March 11, 2026 ⏱ 9 min read 👤 Neuskale Team

Title Search Overview for Mississippi

Mississippi's title search process is shaped by the state's chancery court system, its deed of trust framework, and a recording structure that can vary significantly across its 82 counties. Understanding these nuances is essential for conducting accurate, thorough property title searches in the Magnolia State.

Unlike most states that use a county recorder or clerk of court for land records, Mississippi channels its property recordings through the chancery clerk in each county. The chancery court system handles equity matters including property disputes, making the chancery clerk's office the central repository for deeds, deeds of trust, liens, and other real property instruments.

Recording Systems and County Resources

Mississippi's 82 counties each maintain property records through the chancery clerk's office. The state's larger counties — Hinds (Jackson), Harrison (Gulfport/Biloxi), and DeSoto (Southaven) — generally offer more robust online access to records. However, many smaller rural counties still require in-person searches or have limited digital record availability.

Mississippi uses a race-notice recording statute. Under this system, a subsequent purchaser who records first without notice of a prior unrecorded conveyance will prevail. Title searchers must be thorough in examining all recorded instruments and understand the indexing system used by each county's chancery clerk, as practices can vary between grantor-grantee and tract-based systems.

State-Specific Legal Considerations

Several aspects of Mississippi law directly affect how title searches are conducted and what issues must be examined. These state-specific provisions require careful attention during the search and examination process.

  • Deed of Trust State: Mississippi is a deed of trust state rather than a mortgage state. Property is conveyed to a trustee as security for a loan, and foreclosures proceed through a non-judicial power of sale process. Title searchers must verify the chain of deeds of trust and any substitutions of trustee.
  • Transfer Tax: Mississippi does not impose a state transfer tax on real property conveyances. However, there are recording fees that vary by county and document type.
  • Homestead Exemption: Mississippi provides a homestead exemption that protects up to $75,000 of equity in a primary residence from creditors' claims. The homestead must be properly claimed, and both spouses must join in any conveyance of homestead property.
  • Tax Sale Redemption: Mississippi allows a two-year redemption period after a tax sale, during which the original owner can reclaim the property by paying the taxes plus interest and penalties. Title searchers must verify whether any tax sale redemption periods are still active.
  • Oil and Gas Rights: In southern Mississippi, oil and gas rights are occasionally severed from surface estates. While not as prevalent as in states like Texas or Kentucky, mineral reservations should be checked, particularly in counties with historical petroleum production.

Common Title Issues Found in Mississippi

Mississippi's legal framework and historical land use patterns create several recurring title issues that experienced searchers should anticipate. These include complications with heir property — a significant issue in the state where intestate succession has resulted in fragmented ownership among family members across generations, particularly in rural areas.

Other common issues include unreleased deeds of trust from lenders that have merged or gone out of business, unresolved tax liens from the state's aggressive tax enforcement process, and boundary disputes in areas where original survey markers have been lost. In the Gulf Coast region, post-hurricane rebuilding has occasionally created permitting and zoning complications that appear in the title record.
