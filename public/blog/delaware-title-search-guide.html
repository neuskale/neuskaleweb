Delaware Title Search: A Guide to the First State's Requirements

📅 March 12, 2026 ⏱ 8 min read 👤 Neuskale Team

Title Search Overview for Delaware

Delaware's title search landscape is unique in its simplicity and complexity. As the second-smallest state with only three counties — New Castle, Kent, and Sussex — the geographic scope is manageable, but the state's position as the incorporation capital of America and its distinctive transfer tax structure create specific challenges that title searchers must navigate.

Property records in Delaware are maintained through the Recorder of Deeds office in each of the three counties. All three counties have invested in online record access, making Delaware one of the more accessible states for digital title research. However, the state's dense development patterns in northern New Castle County and the rapid growth of Sussex County's beach communities mean that title histories can be complex despite the small geographic footprint.

Recording Systems and County Resources

With only three counties, Delaware offers a more straightforward research environment than most states. New Castle County (Wilmington) is the most populous and maintains extensive digital records through its Recorder of Deeds office. Kent County (Dover, the state capital) provides online access to land records going back several decades. Sussex County (Rehoboth Beach, Lewes) has seen rapid growth and maintains modernized recording systems to handle the increased volume.

Delaware uses a race-notice recording statute, meaning that a subsequent bona fide purchaser who records first without notice of a prior unrecorded instrument prevails. The state also maintains its UCC filings through the Secretary of State's office in Dover. Title searchers should be aware that Delaware's compact size means that state-level searches (such as judgment liens and UCC filings) can often be completed more quickly than in larger states.

State-Specific Legal Considerations

Delaware's legal framework includes several provisions that are important for title search professionals to understand. The state's favorable corporate laws and transfer tax structure are particularly notable.

  • Realty Transfer Tax: Delaware imposes a realty transfer tax of 4% of the property's sale price, split equally between buyer and seller (2% each) unless otherwise negotiated. In Wilmington, an additional 1.5% city transfer tax applies. This is among the highest in the nation and must be verified during the closing process.
  • Corporate Entity Complexity: Because more than 60% of Fortune 500 companies are incorporated in Delaware, title searches involving corporate-owned properties may require additional verification of entity status through the Delaware Division of Corporations.
  • Mortgage State: Delaware is a judicial foreclosure state, meaning foreclosures must go through the court system. This can create delays in clearing title after a foreclosure and may result in title issues if the process is not properly completed.
  • Homestead Exemption: Delaware does not have a general homestead exemption like many other states. However, there is a limited exemption for bankruptcy proceedings.
  • Ground Rent: While less common than in neighboring Maryland, some older properties in Wilmington and northern New Castle County may still be subject to ground rent arrangements — a leasehold interest in the land beneath a structure. Title searchers must verify whether a property is fee simple or subject to a ground lease.

Common Title Issues Found in Delaware

Delaware's three-county structure and dense historical development create specific patterns of title issues. In New Castle County, older urban properties in Wilmington often carry complex title histories including potential ground rents, outdated easements, and complications from the city's industrial past including possible environmental liens.

Sussex County presents different challenges as coastal properties face issues with flood zone designations, beach replenishment easements, and the rapid pace of new construction creating potential mechanic's lien exposure. Across all three counties, the high transfer tax rate means that title searchers should be alert to any irregularities in prior transaction documentation, as improperly calculated or unpaid transfer taxes can create defects.
